A novel malignant mesothelioma 
organoids-T cell co-culture 
platform for personalized 
immunochemotherapy testing
Yang Liu1,4, Yang Yang2,4, Songlin An3, Hua Wan2, Minghao Zhang2, Ruihong Yin2, 
Weiting Zhao2, Li Huang2, Yunshan Zhao2 & Chenggang Li1
Malignant mesothelioma (MM) is a rare, aggressive cancer with poor outcomes due to its 
heterogeneity and therapy resistance. Patient-derived organoids (PDOs) have emerged as a promising 
tool for precision medicine, as they faithfully recapitulate key tumor characteristics. This study 
aimed to establish MM PDOs and develop a PDO-T cell co-culture system to evaluate their utility 
in guiding personalized MM treatment. MM PDOs were generated from malignant ascites, pleural 
effusions, fine-needle biopsies, and surgical tissues. Organoids were characterized using H&E staining, 
immunohistochemistry (IHC), and next-generation sequencing (NGS). PBMC-derived T cells were co-
cultured with PDOs to assess immunotherapy and chemotherapy responses. Drug sensitivity testing 
was performed to evaluate treatment efficacy. 11 MM PDOs were successfully established, which 
recapitulated the histological features and genomic profiles of their parental tumors. Drug sensitivity 
testing revealed heterogeneous responses of PDOs, with sensitivity to anlotinib but resistance to 
cisplatin plus pemetrexed plus bevacizumab. Notably, PDOs established from the same patient at 
different time points displayed dynamic changes in genetic profiles and drug sensitivities, mirroring 
in vivo tumor progression. In the PDO-T cell co-culture system, anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, especially 
combined with chemotherapy, significantly reduced organoids viability of patient PM002. MM PDOs 
and the PDO-T cell co-culture system constitute robust models for investigating tumor progression, 
drug responses, and immunotherapy efficacy. These tools highlight their potential in advancing 
personalized medicine for MM.
Keywords  Organoid, Mesothelioma, Immunotherapy, Chemotherapy, Drug sensitivity
Malignant mesothelioma (MM) is a rare and highly aggressive cancer that arises from the mesothelial lining of 
the pleura, peritoneum, or other serosal surfaces. It is notoriously difficult to treat due to its late diagnosis, high 
heterogeneity, and resistance to conventional therapies. Approximately 80% of MM cases are associated with 
occupational or environmental asbestos exposure1. The pleura is the most common site of MM (73 to 85%), 
followed by the peritoneum (7 to 18%)2. A large study of the SEER database showed an overall 5-year survival 
rate of 20.3% in patients with malignant peritoneal mesothelioma (MPeM)3. In contrast, the 5-year survival rate 
of malignant pleural mesothelioma (MPM) is only 5%−10%4,5, highlighting the urgent need for more effective 
treatment strategies.
Despite advances in chemotherapy and immunotherapy, the clinical outcomes for MM patients remain poor. 
Platinum-based chemotherapy remains the standard first-line treatment for advanced MM6, but the high degree 
of histological and molecular heterogeneity in MM results in highly variable responses to therapy. Immune 
checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), either alone or in combination with chemotherapy, have shown promise in subsets 
of patients, yet predicting which patients will benefit remains a major clinical challenge7,8. This variability 
underscores the need for more accurate models to predict treatment responses and guide personalized therapy.
Traditional models for studying MM, such as animal models (PDX) and 2D cell cultures, have significant 
limitations. Animal models are time-consuming, expensive, and often fail to fully recapitulate the complexity

These limitations emphasize the need for more accurate and patient-specific models.
Patient-derived organoids (PDOs) have emerged as a promising tool for cancer research and personalized 
medicine. These 3D cultures faithfully recapitulate the genomic and phenotypic characteristics of the patient’s 
primary tumor, can be established rapidly, and have a high success rate. PDOs have been successfully derived 
from various cancers, including colorectal9 and ovarian10 cancers, and have been used in drug sensitivity testing 
to predict clinical responses11,12. However, while mesothelioma organoids have been reported13–16, models for 
tumor-immune cell interactions for preclinical evaluation of immunotherapy in vitro have not been developed.
In this study, we established MM PDOs from diverse clinical specimens and developed a PDO-T cell co-
culture system. We validated that these PDOs recapitulate the histopathological and molecular features of 
parental tumors and demonstrated their utility in testing responses to chemotherapy and immunotherapy. These 
models provide a valuable platform for advancing personalized MM treatment.
Materials and methods
Source of MM specimens
A total of 11 samples were obtained from nine patients. Informed consent was obtained from all participants. 
This study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Darkjade Sciences-Starlite International Science and 
Technology (Beijing) (S2022-148-01), and was performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. All 
experiments were performed in accordance with relevant guidelines and regulations.
Organoid cultures
Organoids were culture as previously described with minor modification15,16. All the samples were transported 
to the laboratory in cold within 4 h after collection. Surgical tumor tissues and fine-needle aspiration specimen 
were washed with phosphate buffered saline (PBS) with 2% penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco, USA) for 15 min, 
and then minced and placed into a dish containing advanced DMEM (Gibco, USA) with TrypLE (Gibco, USA) 
and 2% penicillin-streptomycin in 37 °C for 30 min. The dissociated tumor was then filtered through a 70 
μm cell filter and centrifuged to remove debris. Cell pellets was resuspended in advanced DMEM, and red 
blood cells were lysed with RBC lysis buffer (Invitrogen eBioscience, USA) for 5 min. Tumor cells were washed, 
resuspended in cold MM organoid culture medium with 5% Matrigel (Corning, USA). Then, 200 µL of the cell 
suspension was added into 24-well ultra-low attachment plates. Plates were incubated at 37 °C with 5% CO₂ for 
30 min to allow Matrigel gelation, after which 300 µL of MM organoid medium was added.
For malignant effusion samples, samples were centrifuged at 300 g for 5 min, and the cell pellets were washed 
two times in advanced DMEM/F12 with 2% penicillin-streptomycin. Following centrifuged at 300 g for 5 min, 
cells were resuspended in a cold MM organoid culture medium with 5% Matrigel. Subsequent steps were 
identical to those for tissue specimens.
The MM organoid culture medium was consists of Advanced DMEM/F12 cell culture medium (Gibco) 
supplemented with 1% GlutaMAX (Gibco), EGF (20 ng/mL), 1x N-2 supplement (Thermo Fisher Scientific), 1x 
B-27™ Supplement (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 100 µg/mL of Transferrin human (Sigma), 100 ng/mL of Noggin 
(Peprotech) and 100 ng/mL R-spondin (Peprotech). Medium was refreshed every 2–3 days.
When MM PDOs reach 100–150 μm, passage was possible. The culture medium containing organoids was 
collected into a 15 mL centrifuge tube and centrifuged at 300 g for 3 min. Organoid pellets were resuspended 
in 1 ml of TrypLE and incubated at 37 ° C for 3 min. Dissociated cells were centrifuged at 300 g for 3 min to 
remove the supernatant. The cells were resuspended in organoid medium with 5% Matrigel, and plated at a 1:2 
or 1:3 dilution.
To preserve MM PDOs via cryopreservation, organoids were collected and digested into masses with TrypLE, 
and resuspended in 500 µL fetal bovine serum (FBS) containing 10% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). Suspensions 
were transferred to 1.5 mL cryovials, placed in a freezing container, and stored at − 80 °C overnight. Samples 
were then transferred to liquid nitrogen for long-term storage.
Cryopreserved organoids were rapidly thawed in a 37 °C water bath, transferred to 15 mL tubes containing 
5 mL Advanced DMEM/F12, and centrifuged at 300×g for 3 min. Organoid pellets were resuspended in cold 
organoid medium with 5% Matrigel and plated as described above.
Histological study and immunohistochemistry
Tumor tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 24  h, dehydrated, and embedded in paraffin blocks. 
Organoids were collected by centrifugation at 300 g for 3 min, and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, dehydrated, 
and embedded in paraffin blocks. Paraffin sections were stained with hematoxylin and eosin using standard 
protocols and imaged under a light microscope. Immunohistochemistry (IHC) was performed using antibodies 
targeting the Cytokeratin 5/6 (CK5/6), Cytokeratin 7 (CK7), Calretinin (CR), D2-40, WT-1, Ki67.
Genomic analysis
DNA was extracted from organoids and original tumour samples using the DNeasy Blood & Tissue Kit (Qiagen, 
Germany) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Genomic DNA (200 ng) was fragmented to an average 
of 300 bp, and then subjected to library construction and hybrid capture using a custom-designed panel. The 
custom-designed panel, containing 673 genes, covering 1.1  Mb of the human genomes. The libraries were 
sequenced with 2 × 150 bp paired-end reads on the NovaSeq 6000 (Illumina, Inc., USA) platform.
The raw reads containing adaptor sequence and the reads with low quality (> 50% of bases whose Q scores were 
≤ 10%) were filtered. The clean data were mapped to human reference genome hg19/GRCh37 using Burrows-
Wheeler Aligner (BWA, version: v0.7.17, https://github.com/1h3/bwa.git). Variant calling and annotation

Drug sensitivity testing
MM PDOs were used for drug sensitivity testing 7 days after passage. Organoids were dissociated and 
resuspended in MM organoid culture medium with 5% Matrigel and plated into 96-well plates (50 µl/well, 
4 × 103 cells/well; Corning, USA). After Matrigel gelation, 40 µL MM organoid medium was added per well. 
Organoids were cultured for 48  h, and then treated with serial of concentrations of clinical chemotherapy 
regimens: cisplatin + pemetrexed + bevacizumab (60 µM: 180 µM: 6 µg/mL), cisplatin + gemcitbine (60 µM: 80 
µM), apatinib + etoposide (20 µM: 100 µM), anlotinib (20 µM). All regimens were diluted with dilution factor of 
3 (a total of 10 concentrations). DMSO and Staurosporine were set as the negative control and positive control, 
respectively. The cell viability was assayed using CellTiter-Glo (Promega, USA) after 3 days of drug incubation 
according to instructions by the manufacturer. Results were normalized to the negative control and expressed as 
percentage cell viability. The half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) value was conducted using nonlinear 
regression analysis of the dose–response curve in GraphPad Prism 7.0 (GraphPad Software, USA).
Co-culture of MM PDOs and T cells
To evaluate the effect of anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, we conducted co-culture assays of MM PDOs with T cells. 
T cell derived from PBMC was established as previously described with minor modification17. The PBMC 
fraction was isolated from peripheral blood using Ficoll (Corning), and was cultured in RPMI 1640 (Corning), 
supplemented with 2 mM ultraglutamine I, 25 mM HEPES (pH 7.2), 5.5 × 10−5 M β-mercaptoethanol, IL-2 
(6000 IU/ml; Sigma), and 10% inactivated serum (Gibco). CD3 monoclonal antibody and CD28 antibody 
(eBioscience) were added for T cell activation at least 48 h before co-culture.
For co-culture, T cells were firstly treated with nivolumab (100 µg/mL; anti–PD-1 antibody) or isotype control 
(100 µg/mL, IgG) in 96-well ultralow attachment plates (Corning) for 2 h. PDOs were digested into small cell 
clumps or single cells, and were mixed with T cells at an effector-to-target ratio of 10:1 (5 × 105 : 5 × 104). After 
co-culture for 24 h, the co-cultures were treated with or without chemotherapy drugs (cisplatin + pemetrexed, 15 
µM : 45 µM) for three days. T cells alone were cultured and treated in the same way. T cells cultured alone (with 
identical treatments) served as a control to subtract T cell viability from co-culture viability, enabling accurate 
quantification of PDO viability. The cell viability was assayed using CellTiter-Glo (Promega). Untreated PDO-T 
cell co-cultures served as the negative control.
Statistical analysis
Assays were conducted in technical triplicates across three independent experiments. Statistical analyses were 
performed using GraphPad Prism software. For two-group comparisons, IC50 values were analyzed via T-test. 
For multi-group comparisons of relative differences in cell viability, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) 
followed by Tukey’s post-hoc test was applied. A P-value ≤ 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results
MM PDOs were established from diverse types of samples collected in clinical scenarios
To establish as many MM PDOs as possible, we collected potential samples in a variety of clinical scenarios, 
including effusion, fine-needle aspiration biopsy, and surgical tissue samples. 11 MM PDOs were successfully 
established (Fig. 1 and Table S1), with a 100% success rate across all specimen types. Organoids were formed after 
5–14 days of culture, with slight inter-patient variation in expansion rates. All organoids could be passaged for 
more than three generations, and maintained their original characteristics after freezing and thawing (Fig. 1c). 
MM PDOs exhibited diverse morphologies, ranging from cystic to dense structures with varying circularity and 
cellular cohesion. The morphology of organoids seemed to be unrelated to sample types, suggesting our culture 
method was suitable for samples obtained in various clinical settings.
Notably, we collected effusions from the same patient at different time points (reflecting distinct disease 
stages), enabling the establishment of paired PDO lines: PM002-1O/PM002-2O and PM004-1O/PM004-2O 
(Fig. 1b). These paired lines provide a unique resource for studying tumor evolution and dynamic changes in 
drug sensitivity.
MM PDOs maintained the histological features of parental tumors
To validate the histological fidelity of MM PDOs, we compared H&E staining and IHC assay of PDOs with 
matched parental tumor tissues. H&E staining confirmed that PDOs retained key histological features of MM, 
including polygonal cells with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and glandular/sheet-like arrangements (Fig. 2, 
Fig. S1).
IHC analysis (Fig.  2) showed that PDOs preserved the expression pattern of MM-specific biomarkers: 
positive staining for CK5/6, D2-40, WT-1, and CR (pan-MM markers) and CK7 (epithelioid MM marker). 
Additionally, Ki67 staining confirmed significant proliferative activity in PDOs, consistent with the malignant 
nature of parental tumors. Collectively, these results demonstrate that MM PDOs faithfully recapitulate the 
histological characteristics of their parental tumors.

MM PDOs retained the genomic profiles of parental tumor
To assess genomic fidelity, we performed targeted NGS on three PDO lines and their matched parental tissues 
(PM004, PM005, PM006). We analyzed the top 30 most frequently mutant genes in MM, and generated mutation 
heatmaps (Fig. 3a). Key MM-associated mutations, including those in BAP1, CDKN2A, and MUTYH8, were 
detected in both PDOs and matched patient samples. Comparative analysis of variants revealed a high degree 
of concordance between PDOs and parental tissues: 75% (PM004), 41.2% (PM005), and 53.3% (PM006) shared 
mutations (Fig. 3b). These results confirm that MM PDOs retain the core genomic features of parental tumors.
We further investigated genomic evolution using paired PDO lines from patients PM002 and PM004. PDO 
PM002-2O retained 45% (9/20) of the variants present in PM002-1O, with few new variants emerging (Fig. 3c 
and d). In contrast, PDO PM004-2O shared 75% (6/8) of variants with PM004-1O but acquired additional 
mutations, likely reflecting clonal evolution of the primary tumor. These findings highlight the utility of paired 
PDO lines for studying intra-tumor heterogeneity and tumor evolution.
Drug sensitivity of MM PDOs correlates with clinical therapeutic responses
Given that organoid drug sensitivity has been reported representing the clinical response of anti-cancer therapy 
in other cancers, we evaluated the drug response of MM PDOs with four chemotherapy regimens. Specifically, 
organoids were dissociated into small clumps and seeded into 96-well plates. Cell viability of the PDOs was 
examined after treatment with serial concentrations of drugs. IC50 values were calculated to quantify the drug 
response of different MM PDOs to each test drug.
Drug sensitivity assays revealed both significant similarities and variations in response to the assayed agents. 
Most MM PDOs demonstrated relative sensitive towards anlotinib, but resistant to the standard first-line 
cisplatin + pemetrexed + bevacizumab regimen (Fig. 4a and b). PM001O was more sensitive to most drugs, while 
PM004-2O displayed higher resistance.
We further correlated MM PDO drug sensitivity results with the clinical responses of their matched 
patients. PM004, a 53-year-old male, was diagnosed as peritoneal MPeM, Stage III. He received first-line 
cisplatin + pemetrexed + bevacizumab therapy, and was assessed stable disease (SD) at the sixth and 12th week 
after treatment. But progressive disease (PD) was confirmed by computed tomography (CT) at the 7th month, 
accompanied by increased ascites. Ascites collected at this time was used to establish the PM004-1O organoid. 
Three weeks later, ascites further increased, and a second organoid (PM004-2O) was established from the newly 
collected ascites. Consistent with the patient’s clinical progression, both PM004-1O and PM004-2O showed 
resistance to cisplatin + pemetrexed + bevacizumab. Notably, PM004-2O had higher IC50 values across multiple 
regimens than PM004-1O, reflecting progressive chemoresistance (Fig. 4c).
Patient PM002, a 48-year-old male, was diagnosed as MPeM, stage IV (TxN2M1). Ascites from the patients 
was collected o establish PM002-1O. He received first-line immunotherapy with nivolumab + ipilimumab as the 
treatment, achieving a sustained partial response (PR) for 10 months. Then ascites increased, and PM002-2O 
was established from the new ascites. The patient was then switched to cisplatin + pemetrexed + bevacizumab

Discussion
In this study, we successfully generated MM PDOs from diverse clinical specimens (effusions, biopsies, surgical 
tissues) that faithfully recapitulate the histological and genomic features of parental tumors. By generating paired 
PDO lines from the same patient at different time points, we characterized the dynamic changes in genomic 
alterations and drug responses during tumor progression. Furthermore, we developed a PDO-T cell co-culture 
system to evaluate responses to immunotherapy and chemotherapy. These tools address key limitations of 
traditional preclinical models and provide a valuable platform for advancing personalized MM treatment.
Immune checkpoint inhibitors have emerged as a promising therapeutic option for MM, but response rates 
remain variable, highlighting the need for models that recapitulate tumor-immune interactions. The interplay 
between immune cells and tumor cells plays a pivotal role in determining tumor initiation, progression, and 
response to external influences such as treatment18. Conventional PDOs lack immune components, limiting 
their utility for immunotherapy research. To address this, we co-cultured MM PDOs with PBMC-derived T 
cells, an approach validated in other cancers. For example, co-culture of colorectal cancer organoids with PBMCs 
showed that anti-PD-1 treatment impaired the proliferation of immune-sensitive PDOs19, and co-culturing 
pancreatic tumor organoids with PBMCs induced patient-specific T cell-mediated cytotoxicity20. In our 
system, the anti-PD-1 + chemotherapy combination treatment exhibited enhanced efficacy against MM PDOs, 
mirroring potential clinical benefits. This co-culture model not only advances immunotherapy research but also 
provides a tool for predicting patient-specific responses. However, we also acknowledge the impairment of T

Due to the diversity in collection sites and time points, these organoids can reflect common tumor 
characteristics, as well as distinct features. As showed in this study, MM PDOs from different sample types 
showed little difference in morphology and histology, whereas PDOs from malignant effusion samples showed 
more genomic variation than those from tissue samples. The emergence of new variants found in PDOs may be 
due to the overgrowth of rare clones or differences in sampling sites. By establishing a diverse range of organoids, 
the tumor progression could be monitored and analyzed from multiple perspectives, thereby enhancing the 
options and possibilities for disease research and treatment. Additionally, a comprehensive sample bank would 
opens up more avenues for medical research and disease therapy.
Our study also provides insights into MM tumor progression. Paired PDO lines from patients PM002 and 
PM004 revealed dynamic changes in genomic profiles and drug sensitivity over time. PM004-2O (established at 
a later disease stage) acquired new mutations and exhibited increased chemoresistance, likely reflecting clonal 
selection under therapeutic pressure. These findings highlight the utility of serial PDO lines for studying intra-
tumor heterogeneity and guiding adaptive treatment strategies.
There are some limitations in this study. First, due to the rarity of MM, the number of samples analyzed 
in this study was relatively limited. Larger cohorts are needed to validate our findings. Second, the absence of 
paired tissue and effusion samples from the same patient prevented a direct comparison of organoids derived 
from different sample types. Future studies should address this to optimize PDO establishment protocols. Third, 
further longitudinal studies are needed to confirm the predictive value of PDO drug sensitivity for long-term 
clinical outcomes, which will accelerate the translation of PDOs into clinical practice.
In conclusion, we have established a comprehensive platform of MM PDOs and a PDO-T cell co-
culture system. These models faithfully recapitulate tumor biology, enable evaluation of chemotherapy and 
immunotherapy responses, and hold significant potential for personalized MM treatment.
